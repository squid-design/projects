DOSE

Dose is the anchor of every espresso recipe. It is the weight of dry ground coffee that you are using to make an espresso, and depending on your espresso style, it can be anywhere from 5-30 grams, though in general modern espresso hovers between 18-21g. Deciding on your dose is always the first step to creating an espresso recipe. Before that, you should think about these questions:

  • How much espresso do you want to make?
  • What size cup do you want to extract?
  • What strength do you want to attain?
  • What kind of flavor profile are you looking to achieve?

When we talk about strength, we refer to the total dissolved solids (TDS) within the coffee.
This is not to be confused with the intensity of the coffee, which refers to the magnitude of perceived flavor.

Drip/Filter VS. Espresso

The most efficient way to communicate the strength of coffee is to talk in terms of percentages. Most brewed coffee are typically between 1.2-1.8% coffee to 98.2-98.8% water. Most espressos are typically between 7-12% coffee. Even at such a low TDS, espresso has a strength that is greater than brewed coffee by tenfold. Coffee is an incredibly intense flavoring agent and any small variations in strength are easily identified by our palates.

TIME

Time is the last ingredient in the espresso recipe, which is a more quantitative way of measuring the grind size of the coffee.

These graphs are generated based on various trials at different parameters. They give a general description of how grind size and tamp technique can affect the level of total dissolved solids (TDS) and the balance of flavor. The palate is a strong tool, but these graphs do not provide sufficient evidence due to a lack of quantifiable variables. For that, we must collect some data.

YIELD

Yield is the next ingredient in the espresso recipe, which we will measure in weight. Since espresso is pulled at such low volumes, the idea of trying to measuring yield in terms of volume is erroneous because a misread of 1mm will result in a 5% error. So remember, weight not volume.

A little note about crema:

As a coffee ages after roasting, that carbon dioxide dissipates and the espresso has much less crema. Crema is mostly air and its density is far lower than the espresso liquid below, which makes volume measurements close to meaningless.
Yield is a major battle between strength and extraction. More yield gets you more extraction, but lower strength. Less yield gets you less extraction, but higher strength.

This is a regular 30-second espresso shot. You’ll notice no numbers apart from time. This is deliberate, because this example could be any combination of dose, yield, time, extraction or strength. The specifics don’t really matter. What matters is how the different elements move and change through time.

The First Ever Espresso Calculator

OK. You think you're perfect and have it all figured out. You don't need any help because you can feel the weather and the humidity, can count seconds in your head and weigh the grinds by feel, right?

Wrong - even the amount of time your grinder has been running will affect your flavor profile.
Every shot is an individual snowflake, and it is impossible to map it out and replicate it. Each model of espresso machine is built differently and calibrated for specific cafe settings. By pulling metadata from baristas all over the world, along with espresso reviews, we will be able to fingerprint every shot and help you measure and find those factors that allow you to pull the best shot every time.
